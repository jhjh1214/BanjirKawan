## 3 · THE WEBINAR FRAMEWORKS (everything they taught — use their vocabulary)

The judges listen for their own frameworks reflected back. These all appear in our
analysis; the pitch surfaces the most important ones out loud.

**3.1 · The 6-step build** — the skeleton of the pitch:
`Frame → Analyse → Money → Idea → Fund → Pitch`
- Frame: one flood-prone kedai; keep it running in bad weather.
- Analyse: "what if floods double?" → the last-metre gap widens.
- Money: weather→wallet, four money effects, RM 6.1B.
- Idea: dry-day AI survey → cached playbooks → deterministic alert → claim evidence.
- Fund: three payers (insurers/takaful, councils, kedai).
- Pitch: the whole thing.

**3.2 · The four money effects (SCDL)** — a flood hits all four at once:
`Sales (lost while shut) · Costs (cleanup) · Damaged goods · Loans (costlier; claim denied without evidence)`

**3.3 · Weather → wallet (→ wellbeing)** — the causal chain from a weather event to
money, and (our extension, per the judge) to social cost: livelihood, jobs, debt,
displacement — hitting the most vulnerable hardest.

**3.4 · The four resilience capacities** — "bounce forward":
`Anticipate → Absorb → Adapt → Recover`. BanjirKawan maps 1:1:
- Anticipate = tiered early warning + 3h lead time
- Absorb = ranked action playbook cuts damage
- Adapt = learning loop sharpens each monsoon
- Recover = telemetry-backed claim report ("bounce forward, not just back")

**3.5 · The Iceberg model** — `Event → Pattern → Structure → Mental model`:
- Event: a kedai floods overnight (RM30–80k lost).
- Pattern: the *same* shops flood every monsoon despite warnings.
- Structure: warnings are river-centric (not shop-centric); no pre-loss evidence baseline; the "what to save first" knowledge lives only in the owner's head.
- Mental model: "banjir is fate — you mop and restock." → We install: "a warning is my 3-hour action window, and my loss is claimable."

**3.6 · Design for the worst day, not the average** — build for the failure first.

**3.7 · The Five Marks of a system that holds:**
`Strong · Backups · Variety · Flexible · Bounces back`
- Strong: short deterministic tested critical path.
- Backups: cached playbooks, AI fallback (dry-day only), dispatch retries.
- Variety: Telegram / SMS / paper; 3 languages.
- Flexible: modular; per-station thresholds are config not code.
- Bounces back: recovery mode + learning loop.

**3.8 · The three climate scenarios (NGFS/IPCC)** — stress-test, don't invent:
- Fast & smooth (early action): fewer extremes; we still deliver recovery/claims + keep the practice muscle alive at WATCH tier. Degrades gracefully.
- Slow then sudden (late, panicky): **our sweet spot** — more intense floods, underprepared shops, ≈RM0 marginal cost per event.
- Barely acts (much worse weather): system scales with the climate signal because plans are pre-cached and the storm path has no AI/cost dependency.

**3.9 · The Seven Maxims** (Q&A ammo; not all spoken):
1. Every action has side effects → we removed AI-in-the-storm-path.
2. The cure can be worse than the disease → a resilience tool that fails in the event it exists for is worse than none; failure modes designed first.
3. Structures drive behaviour → lint rule *enforces* "no AI in the critical path" (build fails otherwise); checklist RM-ordering structures 3am panic.
4. The easy way out leads back in → pushing raw warnings reproduces the failure; we took the hard per-shop path.
5. Behaviour grows better before it grows worse → WATCH-tier practice runs prevent complacency before the big flood.
6. Delays make systems oscillate → watchdog for feed lag; immediate metrics bridge the one-season learning delay.
7. You are not the centre of the system → we bolt onto existing JPS/Telegram/takaful; we're the *last metre*, not a replacement.

**3.10 · Climate-U values** (prize framing — the Climate Systems Leadership ethos):
Collaboration/co-creation (participatory onboarding — AI proposes, owner confirms),
interdisciplinarity, community engagement, evidence-based action, long-term
sustainability, inclusivity (3 languages, 3 channels, subsidised access). Pilot framed
as **Participatory Action Research (PAR)**: 20 shops on one Taman Sri Muda street, one
monsoon, measured against the neighbouring street.

**3.11 · The 6-tick "strong solution" bar** (name if it fits):
☑ One clear place · ☑ Asked "what if?" · ☑ Money makes sense · ☑ Systems view that
won't backfire · ☑ A way to pay · ☑ Real & honest.

## 4 · THE JUDGE SIGNAL — social systems (CRITICAL, from mentor Dr. Kevin)

A mentor (Dr. Kevin), echoing Dean Lim, explicitly told us the panel grades the
**holistic, social** systems lens — not just the financial one — and named the panel's
reference book: **David Peter Stroh, *Systems Thinking for Social Change***. Use this
vocabulary out loud; it is the single biggest steer we received.

Stroh's ideas we deploy:
- **Symptoms vs root causes** — go beyond the flooded shop (symptom) to the last-metre gap (root cause).
- **System archetypes — "Shifting the Burden":** relying on post-flood relief + loans (symptomatic quick fix) lets real resilience wither, so the problem returns. We move the intervention **upstream** to anticipation (the fundamental solution).
- **Positive (reinforcing) & negative (balancing) feedback loops** — see §6.
- **Leverage points** — small, well-placed change, big effect = the last metre.
- **Unintended consequences (anticipate the harm):** we named and designed against:
  (a) **insurer redlining** (using risk data to drop/price-out the exposed) → owner owns the data; we sell resilience & verified claims, not risk-scoring for exclusion; work at community/council level to keep the exposed covered;
  (b) **digital divide** → SMS + paper;
  (c) **alert fatigue / dependency** → tiered alerts with a debounce; owner in control.
- **Social externalities & justice/fairness:** floods cost livelihoods, jobs, debt, displacement, and **hit the vulnerable first** (uninsured, informal, women-run micro-shops like Kak Ros). Fairness is a **design principle**, not an add-on: subsidised access for those least able to pay; "a warning you need resources to act on is regressive."
- **Stakeholder engagement / holistic impact measurement** — participatory onboarding & PAR pilot; measure systemic/social outcomes, not just outputs.

## 5 · THE PROBLEM (Problem 1 anchor)

**Problem 1 — "Keep something important running in bad weather."** *"Storms and floods
can shut down the things people rely on — a clinic, a market, a bus route, a water or
power supply. Pick ONE such place and design something that keeps it running when the
weather turns bad. For: shops, clinics, transport, neighbourhoods."* Its worked example
uses a flood-prone clinic and lists **"flood-warning texts"** as the idea; its **systems
angle: "the fix needs power, phones and roads too — solve them together."**

Why BanjirKawan is the textbook answer: it names **shops** first; the idea is literally
shop-level flood warnings→action; and the "power/phones/roads" systems angle is exactly
our three-channel (Telegram/SMS/paper) degraded-mode design.

**The real gap ("the last metre"):** Malaysia already has world-class telemetry —
~200 real-time JPS river stations with published danger thresholds — but the data stops
at an agency dashboard. Between the river gauge and the shop floor is an unbridged gap.
"Sungai Klang at WARNING" says nothing about *your* freezer and *your* next 3 hours.
The upstream (sensors, models) is solved and expensive; everything downstream (flooded
shop, denied claim, panic loan) flows from this one broken link.

**Persona:** Kak Ros, kedai runcit (sundry shop), Taman Sri Muda, Shah Alam. In the
December 2021 floods her nearest station (Sg. Damansara, ~2.9km) crossed WARNING hours
ahead; the warning was published; she lost ~RM 40,000 anyway. Dec 2021 = ~RM 6.1B
national losses.

## 6 · THE PRODUCT — how BanjirKawan works

**Core principle: AI is never in the storm path.**
- **Dry day (AI thinks):** the owner photographs the shop (5 photos). AI vision builds a
  risk map of every asset — its height off the floor, its ringgit value. **The owner
  confirms and corrects every item** (participatory; nothing hallucinated). We
  pre-compute **tiered action playbooks** (WATCH/WARNING/DANGER, in the owner's
  language) and **cache** them.
- **Storm day (dumb & deterministic):** a worker polls JPS; when a station crosses its
  **own published threshold**, the alert path is `threshold → cache lookup → send`. Zero
  AI, zero generation at alert time. Delivered as a ranked, timed Telegram checklist
  with tap-to-tick actions.
- **After the flood (recover):** 5 after-photos → a damage diff → an itemised **claim
  report** whose evidence column is **JPS's own telemetry** (an independent third
  party). Minutes, not weeks. Feeds a learning loop that sharpens the next playbook.

**Two causal loops (systems core):**
- **Vicious reinforcing loop (the problem):** flood → loss → debt → thinner buffer →
  next flood worse → closure/displacement → the neighbourhood loses its shop. Falls
  first on the vulnerable.
- **Virtuous loop (our fix — same loop reversed = the leverage point):** early warning →
  action → less loss → telemetry-backed evidence → faster payout → trust → more shops →
  better data → better playbooks.
- **Balancing loop (with delay):** completed-checklist telemetry tightens playbooks each
  monsoon; the one-season delay is bridged by showing value-protected metrics
  immediately after every event.

**Degraded-mode design ("worst day"):** feed dies → watchdog tells shops to treat heavy
rain as a warning; network dies → the earliest tier fired hours early + a laminated
paper plan on the wall; Telegram down → SMS behind the same channel interface; all AI
down → nothing changes (lint rule guarantees it).

**Reliability / honesty proofs:** ~100 unit tests; parser tested against a saved *real*
JPS response; ESLint `no-restricted-imports` makes "AI in the critical path" a build
failure (structure, not good intentions); shown undamaged-shop photos, the AI honestly
reported zero damage.

**Business model (B2B2C — the shop is the smallest payer):**
1. **Insurers / takaful (anchor):** a completed checklist = a claim avoided; a verified
   telemetry-backed report = a claim in minutes not adjuster-weeks. Priced per policy /
   per claim avoided. Climate is repricing flood risk now → motivated buyers.
2. **State & local councils:** district-wide SME resilience at software cost.
3. **Kedai (freemium):** basic alerts free (adoption); plan + claim features RM 19/month.
- **Unit economics:** one AI call at dry-day onboarding; every storm-time alert is a DB
  lookup + one message → **marginal cost per shop per flood ≈ 0** → scales to the RM 6.1B
  problem without cost scaling.
- **Moat:** site graphs + behavioural completion data no competitor/government dashboard
  has. **GTM:** one takaful + one council pilot, measured against the next street.
- **Defensibility vs a big insurer building it:** the neutral position (aggregates data
  across shops/carriers; river telemetry is a neutral third party) + the data moat.

**Real metrics (from the running system — the "accuracy lock" numbers):**
- RM **10,600–22,200** protected per event
- **3h 12m** warning lead time
- **2.0s** dispatch latency
- **83%** actions completed (5/6)
- **90s** to first action
- claim report in **4 min** (vs weeks)
- **301** live stations monitored; **2** real escalations detected this week

**Tech (context only — do NOT foreground in the pitch):** Next.js 14 + TS web; Node
worker; Railway Postgres; Gemini (dry-day vision/playbooks only); Telegram Bot API;
cheerio/Zod/Vitest. Deployed on Railway. GitHub: `github.com/jhjh1214/BanjirKawan`.
